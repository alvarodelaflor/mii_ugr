Oro City, Colorado

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Oro City, now a ghost town, was an early gold placer mining town in the Mosquito Range of Lake County, Colorado, United States. Oro is the Spanish word for gold.

[edit] History

Gold was discovered in the area in late 1859, during the Pike's Peak Gold Rush. However the initial discovery, where California Gulch empties into the Arkansas River, was not rich enough to cause excitement. In April of 1860, however, a rich discovery was made on California Gulch six miles east of the Arkansas River, and Oro City was founded on the spot. By July of 1860, the town and surrounding area had a population of 10,000. An estimated $2 million in gold was taken out the first summer from California Gulch and nearby Iowa Gulch, but within three years the richest part of the placers had been exhausted, and the population of Oro City was several hundred. Many claims (each measuring 100 feet along the stream) were consolidated, and worked by ground sluicing. A ditch was dug in 1877 to provide water for hydraulic mining, but the hydraulic mining was reported to be unsuccessful.[1]

Placer mining had always been hampered by a heavy brown sand. As early as 1874, one of the miners determined that the brown sand was the lead mineral cerussite, and that the sand also carried high values of silver. Some veins of gold ore had already been found, but following the brown sand to its sources in the bedrock led to the discovery of large and rich silver deposits. The silver started a new and larger rush, and Oro City filled with silver prospectors, but most people went instead to the new city of Leadville nearby. The 1890 census found the population of Oro to be 222.

[edit] Notable residents

  • Horace Tabor was one of the placer miners in Oro City in 1860.

[edit] Geography

The site of Oro City is at 39°14′05″N, 106°15′08″W. The site is in California Gulch, two miles east of Leadville.
